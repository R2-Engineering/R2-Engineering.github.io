The Short Version

I'm a 48-year-old IT engineer with over 25 years of experience, and I don't fit neatly into a job description someone (or AI) wrote in 20 minutes. I'm a problem solver and builder. I analyze complex problems, determine the best solution, and implement that solution. And then I want to move on to the next hard thing. If you need someone to keep the lights on, I'm not your guy. If you are facing a difficult or impossible technical problem and you don't have someone that you can trust to solve it for you — keep reading.


What I Actually Do

I solve complex problems that involve compliance, security, and automation — usually for organizations that need enterprise-grade solutions but don't have enterprise-grade budgets or patience for enterprise-grade nonsense. My most recent project: I led a government contractor through an 18-month CMMC Level 2 certification. This wasn't just a gap-assessment, fix a few things, pass the audit. This was a complete IT overhaul, from top to bottom. I did most of the technical architecture, design, and implementation. While at the same time working with internal operations teams and external MSP to keep the business running while we overhauled everything. Dozens of IT projects completed. Very few business operations disruptions. 3rd party audit passed. Zero remediation items. Not "mostly passed." Not "passed with a few findings." Zero. That project is a good example of what I do well — take something that feels impossibly complex, break it into systems, build those systems, get them across the finish line, and then go find the next impossibly complex thing.



How I'm Wired

I'm neurodivergent. I'm saying that up front because it helps explain who I am and how I work, and I'd rather you know now than discover it later. Here's what that means in practice: I hyperfocus. When I'm deep in a problem — coding a Python integration, mapping a compliance framework, designing an automation workflow — I can go for hours without coming up for air. This is my superpower. It's how I produce the quality of work I produce. But it means I need stretches of uninterrupted time. And often at odd hours. If your culture is strict "be onsite and at your desk at 8am", back-to-back unnecesary meetings, mandatory "social get togethers", I will drown. I'm systematic, not spontaneous. I build mental models before I build solutions. I document as I go. I think in processes. This makes me excellent at deep technical and compliance work and terrible in fast paced environments that value speed, volume, and profitability over quality and doing things the right way. I'm thoughtful, direct, and opinionated. I don't do corporate glad handing or ass kissing. If something is broken, I'll tell you it's broken. If your vendor is ripping you off, I'll call them out. If you have someone in a leadership position that obviously doesn't belong there, I'm going to bring it up. Not because I'm lobbying for their position, but because I can't tolerate people in leadership positions that don't have the rigt skills, experience, and emotional intelligence. Some people find this refreshing. Others find it uncomfortable. You should know which camp you're in before we talk. Again, this is part of who I am and how I'm wired.


What Lights Me Up

  • Walking into something that's never been done before and figuring it out
  • Writing code that eliminates hours of manual work
  • Translating a 400-page compliance framework into something a 15-person company can actually implement
  • The moment a system I built goes live and just works
  • A company culture built around hard work, integrity, and sincere relationships. The kind that attracts the brightest and most interesting people, and has the maturity and leadership to recognize, honor, care for, and retain those people.
  • Working with people who take extreme pride in what they do, where they own their mistakes and put systems in place to avoid repeat mistakes. People that are wired to do things the right way, not just the way they were told to do it, or the way everyone else does.


What Kills Me

  • Maintaining systems someone else built. I respect the work. I just can't sustain energy for it.
  • People management. I've done it. I managed a team that grew from 2 to 20 over 15 years. I learned I'm not wired for it — not because I don't care about people, but because the emotional labor of managing humans is fundamentally different from the problem-solving that energizes me.
  • Performative collaboration. Standing meetings where nothing gets decided. Teams and chats where the real purpose is visibility, not communication.
  • "Culture" that's really just checking a box and adding pizza or donuts.
  • Being asked to sell. I can demo something I built all day long. I can explain technical concepts to non-technical people. But cold outreach, quota pressure, and rejection-as-a-business-model are not things I can do.

What I'm looking for

The Environment Where I Thrive

Remote-first. Not "remote-friendly" where everyone in the office has context you don't. Actually remote-first, where async communication is the default and structured meetings are the exception.


Autonomy. Tell me what the goal is and what needs to be solved and let me work. Don't tell me how someone else or another company did it, and don't expect me to sit at a desk for 8 hours.


Low/No politics. I've worked in environments where the smartest person in the room was whoever talked the most. I've also worked in environments where results spoke for themselves. I only function in the second kind.


Mission I can believe in. I don't need to be saving the world. But I need to believe the work matters and that the people around me actually care about doing it well.


Ownership. I don't need to actually own stock or be an owner on paper. I operate like an owner because I've owned and built a company and know what it takes. This is how I'm wired and not something I can turn off. This should be celebrated and not exploited. I don't ask for raises and I don't expect praise for doing my job. I do expect to be compensated fairly, and I expect the same for my colleagues. I do my best work in places where everyone shares in the success they help create.



What I Bring to the Table

  • 25+ years in IT — spanning industrial automation and validation, engineering, infrastructure, compliance, and business operations
  • CMMC/NIST/HIPAA compliance — I've helped companies navigate all sorts of audits, compliance, and assessments. Either as an advisor, or as hands-on
  • M365 GCC security architecture — the government-grade stuff, not just "we use SharePoint"

What I'm Not

I want to be honest about this because it saves everyone time. I'm not a manager. I'm not a salesperson. I'm not an operator who's going to happily monitor dashboards and close tickets for the next decade. I'm not going to thrive in a culture that values presence over output, consensus over conviction, or speed over quality. I'm also not 25. I'm not going to work 70-hour weeks to prove I'm "passionate." I've already proven what I can do. At this point, I'm looking for a place that wants what I'm good at and doesn't need me to pretend I'm good at the things I'm not.
